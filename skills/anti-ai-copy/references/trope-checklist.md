# AI Writing Trope Checklist

Use this for full copy reviews across product pages, docs, READMEs, emails, posts, bios, and essays. Do not treat it as a blind word blacklist. The test is whether the phrase creates generic polish instead of meaning.

## Hard-cut phrases
Usually remove or rewrite:
- in today’s fast-paced world
- in today’s rapidly evolving landscape
- in the ever-evolving world of
- it is important to note
- it is worth noting
- in conclusion / in summary, when used as boilerplate
- not just X, but Y
- more than just X
- where X meets Y
- the future of X
- game-changing / revolutionary / transformative
- a testament to
- plays a crucial / pivotal role
- when it comes to
- at the end of the day, unless it fits the writer’s real speech

## High-risk verbs
Usually replace the sentence with concrete behaviour:
- unlock
- unleash
- elevate
- empower
- streamline
- optimize, when not technical
- transform
- enhance
- leverage, when “use” works
- delve
- navigate, when used with “complexities”
- harness

## High-risk adjectives
Use only if backed by evidence:
- seamless
- robust
- powerful
- intuitive
- innovative
- cutting-edge
- dynamic
- comprehensive
- effortless
- scalable
- meticulous
- pivotal
- crucial
- vibrant

## Abstraction fog
These often hide the actual meaning:
- landscape
- ecosystem
- journey
- solution
- experience
- platform
- capabilities
- potential
- productivity
- workflow, if repeated without naming the work
- realm
- tapestry
- intersection, when used as “at the intersection of X and Y”

## Email tells
Often cut:
- I hope this email finds you well
- I wanted to reach out
- I am writing to inform you
- please do not hesitate to
- thank you for your understanding
- I would like to take a moment to

Better moves:
- lead with the reason for the email
- ask the actual question
- keep warmth where it is real, not ceremonial

## Social-post tells
Often cut:
- “Here’s the thing:” when it only delays the point
- “The real kicker?” when nothing kicks
- numbered lessons that all have the same cadence
- “This changed everything” without a concrete before/after
- faux vulnerability followed by a pitch

Better moves:
- start with the specific observation
- keep one sharp line, not five
- include a concrete detail or caveat

## Product/marketing tells
Often cut:
- “built for modern teams”
- “all-in-one solution”
- “supercharge your productivity”
- “move faster than ever”
- “designed to help you”
- “without compromising on quality”

Better moves:
- name the actual parts
- show the tradeoff
- admit what it does not do
- make CTAs plain

## AI-ish structure tells
- Balanced paragraphs where every sentence is medium length.
- Three-item adjective stacks: “powerful, flexible, intuitive”.
- Setup-transition-payoff rhythm in every paragraph.
- Over-explaining obvious implications.
- Generic “why it matters” closers.
- One paragraph politely summarizing the previous paragraph.
- Too many em dashes used for sophistication rather than meaning.
- Bullets where every item starts with the same polished verb.

## Better replacement moves
### Name the product nouns
Instead of:
> streamline your development workflow

Use:
> keep composer, terminal, git, and sessions together

### Admit the edge
Instead of:
> seamless cross-platform experience

Use:
> Windows installer, Linux AppImage, Mac hopefully

### Use dry contrast
Instead of:
> not just a coding app, but a new way to build

Use:
> Not an IDE. Not a dashboard. A shell for agent work.

### Cut the throat-clearing
Instead of:
> It is important to note that local dictation runs on CPU.

Use:
> Local dictation runs on CPU.

### Replace promise with mechanism
Instead of:
> empowers developers to collaborate with agents

Use:
> gives agents terminal access, git context, and a composer to work from

### Keep the writer’s odd phrase
If the source has one slightly weird but clear phrase, preserve it. AI-ish editing often removes the only human part.

## Universal house posture
Aim for:
- concrete
- contextual
- voice-preserving
- useful before clever
- comfortable with caveats
- varied rhythm

Avoid:
- VC deck words
- founder guru voice
- launch-thread hype
- fake rebellion
- random slang injected as “humanity”
- flattening every writer into the same dry persona

## Source notes
Patterns collected from current research and writing commentary:
- TechRadar reports leakage of words like “delve”, “realm”, “pivotal”, “meticulous”, phrases like “the real kicker”, and structures like “it’s not X, it’s Y”; it recommends simpler wording and preserving personal quirks.
- Tom’s Guide notes vague claims such as “experts agree” without sources, warns against treating em dashes as proof, and recommends adding caveats and pitfalls.
- How-To Geek describes AI writing as too uniform and unlike how people normally communicate.
- Microsoft Copilot’s writing guidance identifies redundant phrasing, neutral tone, and vague language as common AI-text traits, and recommends bringing key points forward and adding real examples.
- Louis Bouchard’s AI-editing guide points to phrases like “delve into”, “underscore”, “ever-evolving landscape”, “seamless, robust solutions”, and stock email openings as common model-polish.
- Tropes.fyi catalogs recurring AI patterns across word choice, sentence structure, tone, formatting, and composition.
- Winston AI and Pangram publish common AI phrase/category lists; use them as heuristics, not proof.
- Reddit writing communities repeatedly flag smooth sameness, “not just X, it’s Y” structures, “in today’s world”, “moreover”, and over-polished vocabulary, while warning that no single word proves AI authorship.
